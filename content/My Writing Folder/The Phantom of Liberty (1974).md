Social norms and taboos turned inside-out, Bunuel’s network narrative floating from one thread to the next like a game of free association. Dozens of unrelated characters are met on the way, but the real concern of the vignettes lies in the everyday constructs guiding their behaviour. Whereas _The Discreet Charm of the Bourgeoise_ (1972) had one overriding target of satire and a cumulative effect to its escalating strangeness, this is naturally more of a mixed bag: a friendly gathering in a hotel inn disrupted by a dominatrix, who flagellates a man in bottomless trousers, is uproariously funny; later, a police academy lecture is ignored by snickering policemen who behave like schoolchildren, which is a little more routine. “I am sick of symmetry,” declares one figure, but what impresses is how symmetrical it all is; there’s enough repetitions, call-backs, and coincidences to give the surrealism a kind of mad, circular logic.
