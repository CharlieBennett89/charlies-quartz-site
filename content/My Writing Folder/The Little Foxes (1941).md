What a year 1941 was for Gregg Toland. After shooting _Citizen Kane_ for Orson Welles, the genius cameraman re-teamed with William Wyler to translate Lillian Hellman’s blackest play for the silver screen, which cemented his pioneering deep-focus style. No attempt is made to sweeten the avaricious Regina Giddens (Bette Davis), whose stony neglect of her ailing husband (Herbert Marshall) and her casual deception of her siblings over a family fortune are shown in the sharp clarity of Toland’s complex compositions, pressing everyone together in shots both long and wide. Patricia Collinge’s alcoholic Birdie retreating into her childhood memories as the rest of the family visibly ignores her; Theresa Wright’s wide-eyed teen finding herself swallowed up in the Xanadu-like mansion of Gothic staircases and opulent mirrors; an open drawer jutting out from the edge of the frame, pointing at an anxious Dan Duryea like a dagger. Has intra-familial deceit ever been shown in images so vivid, so piercing, so clear-eyed? “That’s cynical. But cynicism is an unpleasant way of telling the truth.”
