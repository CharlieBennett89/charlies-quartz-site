This might be the least doom-ridden noir ever made. Don't expect Robert Siodmak's ethereal expressionism or Sam Fuller's hard-nosed brutality; it's a Howard Hawks movie through and through, which means a breezy tone, verbal ping-pong and insolent dames. Fast-talking, quick-thinking P.I. Philip Marlowe (Humphrey Bogart) usurps control of each scene, making his sparring partners' heads spin so much they implicate themselves, ("So you _have_ got that picture?") and untangles a mystery so oblique it's best treated as a distraction. Hawks’ real interest is how these characters’ look, how they move, and how they enunciate Raymond Chandler’s hard-boiled dialogue so it sounds more casual, even Groucho Marxian, with its wordplay. (“She tried to sit on my lap while I was standing up.”) A riddle of digressions and little details, where silver-tongued chatter, shifting relationships and vivid gestures appear as cryptic as most other films’ use of shadows.
