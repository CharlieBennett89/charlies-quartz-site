A star-making turn for Alain Delon, whose Tom Ripley lies and murders his way up into the lap of luxury without a flicker of remorse. René Clément adapts a Patricia Highsmith novel with Melville's regular cinematographer Henri Decaë, but it'd be a mistake to call this a noir: the luscious blue seas and sun-drenched Italian villages look too enticing, too vibrant. And emplaced among the radiant sights is Delon, wearing designer clothes and sailing through scenes with the confidence of a catwalk model; the loner criminal transformed into a socialite, a sensualist, an _homme fatal_. If Anthony Minghella's *The Talented Mr Ripley* (1999) embodies the chronic stress and uncertainty of a Ripley revised as a callow striver, then Clément's approach is one of composed restraint, its steady pacing and studied framing cleanly aligned with its protagonist's icy caution under the hot sun. Also unlike Minghella's version is an eschewal of humanising psychology; here, the focus is all on the sumptuous surfaces, as if behind Ripley's steely-eyed blankness there lurks nothing at all.
