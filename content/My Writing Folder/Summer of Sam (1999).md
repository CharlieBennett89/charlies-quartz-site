New York swept up in a tide of paranoia, prejudice, and punk rock in one blistering summer, as rendered by a going-for-broke Spike Lee. It’s 1977 and the Son of Sam’s murders rise with the temperature. A philandering Catholic (John Leguizamo) and a closeted outsider (Adrian Brody) both dare to dance their fears away, as the rest of the Bronx frets over inflamed social tensions. A restless, volatile energy simmers within Spike Lee's trademark narrative quilting; from the cops liaising with the mob to vigilantes conspiring in pizzerias to the misfits' revelling at CBGB, the ensemble's stories all intertwining into one fluid, feverish vision, both tightly threaded yet on the verge of spilling over into directorial excess. What’s more, the era’s toxic masculinity, tabloid broadcasting, the ’77 blackout and its ensuing crimewave are also crammed in amidst all the inexhaustible human stories. Oh, and there’s a talking dog. This is Spike Lee at his dizzying best.
