Robert Bresson’s lean depiction of a Resistance fighter’s escape from a Nazi prison camp peels away the theatrics, scrutinises the smallest actions, and crystallises the humanity at the heart of the event. All we know of Fontaine (François Leterrier) is that he is sentenced to death for crimes against Vichy. In lieu of backstories, we have his public and private routines, each one rich in sensuous detail: the scrubbing of hands under a running tap, the careful tearing of fabrics to be wrung into a rope, the scratching and scraping of the wooden cell door with a spoon-turned-chisel. Each action, each glance or gesture, carries the starkness of a ceremony, always burdened with existential weight. Bresson's sense of economy – which includes the substitution of sound over image, and the delivery of dialogue with the sparest of emotion – sharpened with *Diary of a Country Priest* (1951), but the ‘Bressonian’ style was fully realised here, a masterwork so engrossing, so suspenseful, one can miss just how stylistically unusual it really is.
