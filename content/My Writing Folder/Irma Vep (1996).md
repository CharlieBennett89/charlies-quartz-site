As in many works by Olivier Assayas, it's a snapshot of a here and now that has since receded into the past, yet still pulses with a present-tense immediacy. Maggie Cheung, playing a fictionalised version of herself, is to star in a remake of Louis Feuillade's silent serial _Les Vampires_ (1915-16), adapted by 1960s-style _auteur_ René Vidal (Jean-Pierre Léaud) and for an increasingly globalised (i.e. Americanised) cinema of the 1990s. It's French cinema in transition, then, and this state of flux is inscribed onto _Irma Vep_’s erratic, slippery shape. The co-worker gossip, desirous glances, and sub-plots swirling around Cheung are left tantalisingly unresolved; as French as the 'talking films' of Benoît Jacquot or Jacques Doillon, but caught in Assayas' roving camera, their lives feel as unfixed as those directed by Wong Kar-wai. Call it a critique of transnational French cinema, if you like, but above all _Irma Vep_ looks on bemusedly at the upheavals, always wary of change and yet always ready to roll along with the hastening flows of life.
