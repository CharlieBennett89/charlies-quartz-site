A live-wire performance entombed within layers of black and a hushed musical score, less of a neo-noir mystery than the tracing of one woman’s tentative creeping towards self-definition. Jane Fonda navigates a whirlwind of monologues as Bree, who’s neither a hooker with a heart of gold or a Gloria Steinem-esque bootstrapper but simply someone trying to get through each day. “What I’d really like to do is be faceless and bodiless. And be left alone.” Fiercely independent yet habitually caught in the male gaze, she’s a bundle of neuroses unravelling in compositions awash in angular shapes, and pursued by detective John Klute (Donald Sutherland), a man so square all he lacks is a pair of spectacles. Piecing together murder clues with a suburbanite is no one’s idea of a good time, but it’s better than drifting into those cold, empty spaces: the pot-scented bordellos and the sterile corporate boardrooms alike informs and complements Gordon Willis’ expressionist lighting, which in turn articulates the quiet lovelessness of both.
